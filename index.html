pencil, to pen, to paint
- an exploration
of mediums


an investigation of the medium of pencil, pen, and (oil, acrylic, watercolor) paint,
and the intersection that exists between them

context

growing up, I was always taught that the process of creating a painting was as follows: 1. make a sketch of what was going to be painted 2. finalize the sketch 3. lightly erase the pencil sketch so that you could only see faint lines on the canvas 4. begin painting.

works of pencil were typically more present in the preparation part of the process and seemed to present a more “unfinished” look, and the paint was meant to complete and polish the piece.

thus, it always seemed that on paper, the two mediums served completely distinct roles and had little room to coexist. but should that be the case? this project was an investigation on the medium of pencil, pen, and (oil, acrylic, watercolor) paint, and the borders they share and the lines that divide them.


methodology

for each of my pieces in this project, I started by creating a pencil outline of the scene as usual. then, instead of lightly erasing the pencil lines, I directly place paint onto the focal points of the piece. then, at the points of the intersection between the two mediums, I use a ballpoint pen to work over the pencil marks.

works

these are the pieces I created for the project, varying in size, canvas surface, and paint medium used

work one:
main piece


[Oil, Pen, Pencil on Canvas] 2020
20 in x 14 in


This painting depicts a scene similar to the one of my hometown back in China. After coming to America, I frequently felt pangs of nostalgia and would put myself back into the moment in time when I was at my grandmother's house. In that way, this work is a play on memory. From right to left, it illustrates the image of my childhood home forming while going from left to right, it seems as if the colorful memories are fading away. In addition, with this piece, I wanted to experiment with the wooden surface and observe how different mediums interact with each other. The transitions between pencil, pen, and oil imitate the creation process which starts with light sketches, then micron outlining, and finally paint application— a parallel for the puzzle pieces of memory that build upon each other as they form.

Analysis

This piece utilized the pencil to pen to paint transition to create the illustion of memories fading.
However, some thought the piece was unfinished due to the pencil sketches still present in the piece.

reflection

after exploring the boundaries of mixed media, more specifically,
the usage of pencil, pen, and paint in the same piece,
I was able to conclude that not only can the three medium
co-exist, they enhance each other visually and tell a
deeper story that simply using one medium cannot.
